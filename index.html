01 / Setup structural

What IMFA actually is.

Indian Metals & Ferro Alloys is the largest fully integrated ferrochrome producer in India, accounting for roughly 20% of domestic FeCr output and 25% of FeCr exports per company filings. Ferrochrome is a metallic alloy used to make stainless steel — every 100 kg of stainless steel needs 20–25 kg of chromium, almost all delivered through ferrochrome. IMFA's edge: it does not buy chrome ore from the market for the bulk of its needs and does not buy power from the grid. Sukinda + Mahagiri mines (~700 kt FY25 production, ramping to 800 kt FY26 guidance and 900 kt Phase-1 by 2026, then 1.2 Mt Phase-2). 204.5 MW of captive coal-fired power including 4.5 MWp of solar.

This vertical integration is a cost moat, not a pricing moat. Ferrochrome is a global commodity priced in USD/tonne — IMFA cannot dictate price. But its cash cost per tonne sits at the lower end of the Indian curve. So when the cycle turns down, peers bleed first. ICRA's December 2025 rationale captures the same idea: IMFA benefits from the upward shift in the cost curve for leading global producers.

The story today is the biggest capex of its life, executed in parallel. Capacity moves 284,000 → roughly 484,000 tpa by FY28 via two routes: a ₹900 Cr greenfield smelter at Kalinganagar (Furnace-1 commissioning June 2026 per company guidance) and the ₹707.26 Cr Tata Steel Jajpur acquisition (closed 27 February 2026 — base consideration ₹610 Cr at signing, final ₹707.26 Cr at close). Two of the four Tata furnaces restarted on 11 March 2026; two more came online from 16 March 2026.

Where margin is made and where risk sits

Margin is made between captive ore + power and the global FeCr realisation. Risk sits in the FeCr realisation itself (USD-priced, China-and-Indonesia-driven), in the coke / reductant line which is bought from market and follows met-coke imports, and in the export-mix shift if Indian anti-dumping reroutes domestic mills toward IMFA's tonnes (a positive shock).

Where IMFA realisation comes from

FeCr is now negotiated monthly by Chinese mills (Tsingshan, Baosteel, TISCO) since the European benchmark was discontinued in June 2024. South Africa's energy crunch keeps the global cost curve high, which is the structural tailwind ICRA flags.

06 / China Angle quarterly

What China is doing.

What is China doing? quarterly

  • Largest FeCr producer (~5.2 Mt) — but also largest consumer (~3.7 Mt SS in March 2026 alone).
  • FeCr is not being dumped into India; the dumping risk is downstream stainless steel from China + Indonesia + Vietnam (DGTR investigation initiated 29 Sep 2025).
  • Tsingshan tender prices firmed in April 2026 — +150 yuan/T vs March; mill restocking continues.
  • China SS exports fell 35.2% YoY in Q1 2026 — domestic demand absorbing supply, some support for FeCr.

07 / Indian Government Angle annual

Policy levers.

Tailwind

DGTR anti-dumping probe — SS 300/400 series reported

Investigation initiated 29 Sep 2025 against China · Indonesia · Vietnam. Period of investigation Apr 2024–Mar 2025. Final order pending; if it lands, Indian SS mills shift toward domestic FeCr suppliers.

Tailwind

Captive mining leases secured reported

Sukinda 2049, Mahagiri 2055. Auction-based regime would not affect IMFA over the dashboard horizon; it does affect non-captive peers buying ore.

Watch

30% chrome ore export duty reported

India retains a 30% duty on chrome ore exports — it protects domestic FeCr smelters. A duty cut would negate part of IMFA's mining edge.

Watch

India's coke import quotas + ADD reported

Quantitative restrictions on low-ash met coke and provisional ADD findings (Nov 2025) raise input cost for FeCr smelters that depend on imported coke. Impact partially absorbed if domestic met coke prices reset; flag as input-cost risk.

Neutral

No PLI for ferro alloys directly

Indirect benefit if PLI / capex on stainless steel downstream lifts demand. Not an IMFA-specific lever.

08b / Realisation sensitivity quarterly

What every $100/t means.

?

How to read this chart

Ferrochrome is a commodity sold in USD per tonne on Cr-content. The selling price (the realisation) goes up and down with the global cycle.

Math at full FY28 run-rate: ~484,000 tonnes × $100/tonne × ~₹94.5/USD ≈ ₹457 Cr per year impact on revenue at the gross-tonne level. EBITDA flow-through is lower if some of the move passes to selling-mill negotiations.

Assumption: volume held flat at FY28E ~484 kt; pass-through 100%; tax + interest + depreciation held flat. Impact is on revenue and EBITDA; PAT impact ~75% of EBITDA at 25% effective tax.

19 / Final Summary quarterly

The bottom line.

Cyclical commodity producer transitioning into a structurally larger, lower-cost player.

The next 12 months are about ramp execution, not capex announcement.

By FY28, capacity is ~70% larger than today, mining is ~30% larger, and India's #1 ferrochrome producer becomes one of the world's top 10. The greenfield Furnace-1 (June 2026) is the next concrete proof point. The Tata acquisition is closed, the four furnaces are firing, and the leverage profile remains strong (net cash Sep 2025; even with the ₹707 Cr Tata cash spend, balance sheet stays comfortable).

The bull case: DGTR ADD final order + FeCr stays at mid-cycle + capacity ramps on schedule + Glencore Lion stays partial.

The bear case: Glencore Lion full restart by Q1 FY27 + Indonesia SS flood + India coke ADD lifts cost + a softer Chinese SS = FeCr realisation cracks.

Valuation: 1-year return of +166% has compressed the cushion. At ~18× P/E TTM with 52-week high printed today, the market already prices in successful FY28 execution. Downside scenarios re-rate the multiple back to mid-cycle 8–11×.

What to watch each quarter

  1. FeCr realisation per tonne (USD/t Cr basis) and Tsingshan tender price
  2. Net debt level (was negative Sept 2025; trending up post-Tata)
  3. Greenfield Kalinganagar Furnace-1 / Furnace-2 commissioning status
  4. Production volume ramp at ex-Tata Jajpur
  5. Management commentary tone (capture exact quote per concall)
  6. South Africa power tariff news; Glencore Lion ramp pace
  7. DGTR final order on SS dumping
